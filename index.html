Dreams and Success:

A look at

Topic

Dreams, Goals, Aspirations

Dreams and desires are an almost essential part of living. People begin fantasizing about their ambitions starting in their first few years of life, with so many children all across the world dreaming of becoming astronauts, archaeologists, scientists, etc. But whether or not following or even having these dreams is a sensible thing to do is a question that countless philosophies or self-help books have attempted to answer. Dreams can cause people to act irrationally and possibly destroy their lives, or they could turn out beautifully and give a person a true sense of meaning to their life. This portfolio aims to provide you with different perspectives on the worthwhileness of pursuing your ambitions through looks at different media and a historical event.

"The Great Gatsby", by F. Scott Fitzgerald

The Story

"The Great Gatsby" is a story about the American Dream, love, and class in America. The story follows Nick Carraway after he moves to the West Egg district of Long Island, New York from the Midwest. Nick's neighbor, Jay Gatsby, throws very extravagant parties with the intent of showing off his incredible wealth. Nick later learns that Gatsby hosts these parties with the intent of attracting the attention of Daisy Buchanan, Nick's distant cousin. Gatsby and Daisy were once lovers, but Gatsby did not have the means to support them financially, so he has spent the past couple decades accumulating and displaying his wealth. In the end, Gatsby attempts to take Daisy away from her husband, but his aspirations ultimately fail and he dies unhappy, alone, and forgotten.

Laced throughout the story and all of its characters are dreams and aspirations, but in the end they all die or fall short. Gatsby's primary reason behind acquiring his wealth and throwing parties was so that he could end up with Daisy Buchanan. Unfortunately for Gatsby, his plans do not come to fruition, and he is left alone. The despair of failed dreams and hopeless aspirations represents the decline of the American Dream in the society that Fitzgerald has created and in real-life society.





The Author

F. Scott Fitzgerald was born in St. Paul, Minnesota on September 24, 1896. Fitzgerald was named after Francis Scott Key, the author of the "Star-Spangled Banner." Fitzgerald's father was an unsuccessful aristocrat/salesman, and his mother worked at a local wholesale grocer. Since he was young, Fitzgerald has been writing, with his stories or articles being posted in his school's newspaper or in various magazines. This was not perfect, however, Fitzgerald was placed on academic probation and he later dropped out of school to join the Army. Before he had to report for duty, he quickly wrote a novel called "The Romantic Egotist" and submitted it to be published. It was rejected, but the reviewer encouraged him to continue writing. After the World War I, Fitzgerald continued writing and has had many of his works published, including "The Great Gatsby" in 1925.

Poetry Connection

"Audition (The Fools Who Dream)",
from "La La Land"

My aunt used to live in Paris. I remember, she used to come home and tell us these stories about being abroad and I remember she told us that she jumped into the river once, barefoot

She smiled...

Leapt, without looking
And tumbled into the Seine
The water was freezing
She spent a month sneezing
But said she would do it again

Here's to the ones who dream
Foolish as they may seem
Here's to the hearts that ache
Here's to the mess we make

She captured a feeling
Sky with no ceiling
The sunset inside a frame

She lived in her liquor
And died with a flicker
I'll always remember the flame

Here's to the ones who dream
Foolish as they may seem
Here's to the hearts that ache
Here's to the mess we make

She told me:
"A bit of madness is key
To give us new colors to see
Who knows where it will lead us?
And that's why they need us"

So bring on the rebels
The ripples from pebbles
The painters, and poets, and plays

And here's to the fools who dream
Crazy as they may seem
Here's to the hearts that break
Here's to the mess we make

I trace it all back to then
Her, and the snow, and the Seine
Smiling through it
She said she'd do it again

Analysis

"Audition" is about a woman telling a story about her aunt and how she admires her aunt for doing what she wanted and pursuing her dreams. She uses various types of figurative language in order to describe her aunt and show just how much she admires her. The narrator of this poem, Mia, has a very appreciative and admiring tone, almost similar to the tone that Nick had had towards Gatsby near the end of the story, though a closer look shows just how different the tones are. While both narrators appreciate some aspect of their respective person, Mia's appreciation is genuine and has good intentions. Nick, on the other hand, appreciates Gatsby for his hope and how he sees the world, but in the end Nick didn't truly appreciate Gatsby as a person, saying that he was better than a whole group of rotten people put together. Overall, both stories approach the ideas of dreams and what pursuing them might entail, with "Audition" taking a more hopeful and optimistic tone while "The Great Gatsby" has a more negative and pessimistic view.

"Black Wall Street"

The Greenwood District of Tulsa, Oklahoma, more colloquially known as "Black Wall Street", was a district of Tulsa that was very prosperous as a Black economic and cultural center. Unfortunately, it all came to a sudden stop on May 31, 1921. On that day, a mob of white people made their way through the district and burned and looted schools, hospitals, stores, and various other buildings in the city.

Connection

The tragedy that occured in "Black Wall Street" in some ways parallels the message of "The Great Gatsby." The people of "Black Wall Street" had been living content and happy, but it all came down in one fell swoop. Gatsby's death and the events in the later part of the book mirror the way that "Black Wall Street" was decimated. Gatsby had been living with the sole desire to be with Daisy Buchanan in the future. Unfortunately, Fitzgerald's society does not let this happen, and Gatsby's dreams are ripped away from him in just a few short weeks. Both "The Great Gatsby" and the Tulsa Race Massacre also represent how modern society, or at least society in the early 1900s, does not allow people to succeed and fulfill some "American Dream." Modern society is not a meritocracy, and both subjects exemplify that.
